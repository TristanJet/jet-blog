In this essay, I attempt to describe the process of *productive creativity*. A general process of determining where to apply creativity to best produce something tangible. I will use the term, *the product*, to refer to the result of *productive creativity*.

- Taleb's teleological argument
We do not *know* where we are going. You cannot predict prospectively what you wish to make. You can only loosely define the outcome.

But you *can* robustly define where you *start*. The bottom can be defined.

##### *What do I have access to? Is it possible, in principle, to transform it into what I want?*

These questions in conjunction can be asked indefinitely until a satisfactory bottom-level is found.

Once the bottom-level is decided upon the transformation can commence. The process of transforming can be described as tinkering, experimentation and iteration. Exercising *optionality*. 

As I have discovered in the process of writing this essay, what I *want* can change in the middle of the transformation.

I have found that in many creative endeavours there emerges *macro-concepts* which have a significant effect on the product. These are often not fundamental nor well-defined enough to be bottom-level, and treating them as such can actually lead to unproductive constriction. Macro-concepts are often sufficiently vague and abstract such that they can lead to harmful misunderstanding. "It is easier to macobullshit than microbullshit" - Nassim Taleb. However, complete ignorance to macro-concepts, appears to me, to lead to predictable failure.

*Taste*, appears to me, to describe an understanding of such macro-concepts. 

Let us return to the example of programming. Code structure would be an emergent macro-concept of programming. Many have come up with differing "paradigms" to organize code: object oriented and functional programming are the most well-known of these paradigms. These paradigms are not fundamental and sticking to one or the other religiously might not best benefit the product. This is why ultimately software tends to be rewritten in more fundamental languages like C or Rust. These macro-concepts *must* have truth to them, otherwise they wouldn't become such dominant memes.

This is where taste comes in. Taste, to my understanding, is the ability to  use macro-concepts suitably to fit the needs of the product. Taste doesn't have a reliable theory, it often involves qualitatively understanding paradigms. It seems to be developed by implicitly being familiar with other uses of such paradigms.

I'll try my best to identify and give examples of fundamental vs. emergent concepts in other creative mediums which I am *not* deeply knowledgable of.

| Medium  | Fundamental (micro)          | Emergent (macro)      | Product                                |
| ------- | ---------------------------- | --------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Coding  | Language, DSA, computability | Programming paradigms | fast/reliable software                 |
| Sports  | Rules, Physical ability      | Strategy              | winning                                |
| Music   | ???                          | genre                 | pleasant sound                         |
| Writing | Language                     | genre                 | a readable work that conveys something |
As of yet, I also cannot confidently place music theory as fundamental to music.

### The Creativity / Fun Zone
Become intimately familiar with the bottom layer which you seek to transform. Understand the logical bounds of the domain you wish to create in.

The fun zone exists above the fundamental layer. 

I recently finished and shared my first ever **open-source software project: muzi**. It is something that I built from scratch to satisfy my desire and I am very proud of it. Upon finishing muzi I realised: "I am pretty good at this." *I am good at designing and creating software.* I am familiar enough with the programming language **Zig** that I can open a text file and *create*. For the first time in my life, I feel that I am actually skilled at something, and it's *fun*. Manipulating a small pocket of the universe is *fun*.
